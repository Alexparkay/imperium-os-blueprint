Why a chat and a folder, not an app

An app gives you buttons someone else chose. This gives you a colleague with hands: it reads and writes files, runs tools, browses, drafts, and researches, shaped entirely by what your business needs. The button list is whatever you can say.

The folder is the other half of the trick. Everything the system knows lives in plain text files on your machine: you can open any of them, read them, and correct them. No database or server to maintain on day one (a dormant operating layer ships in the box for live operational data, inert until you activate it), and no vendor that can lock you out of your own operations. And because the folder is under version control, every change ever made is recorded and reversible — an undo button for your entire company brain.

That combination — a reasoning engine plus a transparent file system — is what makes this an operating system instead of a chatbot. A chatbot forgets you when the tab closes. This one files what you said before the conversation ends.

The chat is first-class. Dashboards are windows.

A question people ask early: "where do I actually do things?" The answer is always the chat. Pages like the setup tracker — and the live company dashboard built later — are read-outs. They show; they never do.

Rule of thumb: if you're tempted to click, say it instead. "Open my setup page", "what's left to connect", "update the dashboard" all work as sentences.

Life of a message

What actually happens between you hitting enter and work appearing. Say you type: "Had a great call with Sarah from Northgate, she wants the proposal by Friday — also remind me to chase the invoice."

1

Parse

The message is split into every distinct ask: a new contact, a deadline, a deliverable, a reminder. Nothing in a ramble gets dropped.

2

Route

Each ask is matched against the skill library. Proposal drafting hits the drafting workflow; the reminder hits the task board. No skill match? It does the work directly, and flags if this should become a skill.

3

Remember

Sarah lands in the people file, Friday on the calendar, both tasks on the kanban — immediately, before any drafting starts.

4

Execute, under the rules

The proposal draft loads your voice guide, the no-AI-smell rule, and the quality gate: it scores its own output harshly and iterates before you ever see it.

5

Commit & back up

Every file change is committed and pushed to your private backup. The machine could die tonight and you'd lose nothing.

6

Notify

If it was a long job, your phone buzzes when it's done. You were never required to watch.

The compounding part

How it learns

Software normally decays: the team changes, the docs rot, the tool drifts out of date. This system is built to run the opposite direction, through one loop:

use it→ notice friction→ correct it in chat→ the correction becomes a rule or skill edit→ weekly self-check catches drift→ you approve, it commits↩

Three sentences are the levers: "Don't do X again" permanently changes behaviour. "Remember this:" permanently files knowledge. "Make this a skill" turns today's one-off into tomorrow's standard. Every one of them is an edit to a text file you can read — which is why the improvements stick.

A weekly health check scores the Four C's — Context (does it know the business?), Connections (is it plugged in?), Capabilities (can it do the work?), Cadence (does work happen on rhythm?) — so you watch the system climb instead of guessing whether it's working.
